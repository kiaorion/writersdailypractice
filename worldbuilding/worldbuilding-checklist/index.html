A Worldbuilding Checklist for Writers Who'd Rather Be Writing

Kia Orion | March 6, 2026 | 9 min read

Most worldbuilding checklists read like a census form. Here are the questions that actually change how your story feels.

What your characters eat tells readers more about the world than any map legend. A peasant chewing on salted root vegetables says feudal scarcity. A street vendor hawking fried crickets says tropical abundance and a culture comfortable with insects. You can skip the three-page geography lesson if you get the first meal right.

The language of insults reveals the culture's deepest fears. In a seafaring world, the worst thing you can call someone involves cowardice on the water. In a world where magic is hereditary, the slurs target bloodlines. Terry Pratchett understood this better than almost anyone. The insults in Ankh-Morpork in Guards! Guards! tell you everything about a city that runs on corruption and grudging tolerance, where calling someone a "nobby" is somehow worse than calling them a thief because at least thieves have a guild and pay their dues.

Weather is free worldbuilding. A sentence about humidity does more atmospheric work than a paragraph about the political system.

If magic exists, someone has tried to regulate it. If nobody has, that tells you something about your world's relationship with power, and you should know what that something is. I wrote more about how to think through these consequences in the magic systems guide.

Every world has something it's running out of. Scarcity is the engine of every civilization, fictional or otherwise. In N.K. Jemisin's The Fifth Season, the scarce resource is stability itself. The ground won't stop shaking. And because the ground won't stop shaking, every cultural norm, every piece of architecture, every relationship between parent and child bends around that single geological fact. She didn't build a world and then add earthquakes. She started with the earthquakes and let everything else grow from the cracks.

Your world's transportation system determines its politics. If it takes six weeks to cross the kingdom by horse, the king doesn't actually rule the outer provinces. He just thinks he does. If teleportation exists, centralized power becomes almost inevitable. The speed at which information and bodies can move is the single biggest variable in how any society organizes itself, and most worldbuilding guides bury it under "miscellaneous."

If your world has a sun, it has agriculture. If it has agriculture, it has seasons, calendars, and festivals. Pull one thread and the whole thing unspools, which is actually the good news because it means you don't need to invent everything from scratch, you just need to pick the right thread and follow it honestly.

I'm still not sure whether it's better to know your world's history before you start drafting or to discover it as you write. I've tried both. The pre-built history gives you confidence but sometimes feels like a cage. The discovered history feels alive but sometimes contradicts itself by chapter nine. I suspect the answer depends on the writer, which is the kind of non-answer that used to frustrate me but now feels honest.

What does your world do with its dead? This single question generates religion, architecture, and taboo in about ten minutes of thinking. Burial means land use conflicts. Cremation means a relationship with fire that shapes everything from fuel economies to spiritual metaphor. Sky burial means birds are sacred. Pick one and watch the implications ripple outward.

Ursula K. Le Guin once wrote, "The artist deals with what cannot be said in words. The artist whose medium is fiction does this in words." That tension sits at the center of worldbuilding. You're trying to make a reader feel the weight of a place that doesn't exist by arranging sentences on a page. The checklist is really a set of pressure points where language can conjure sensation.

The difference between a world and a setting is time. Settings are static. Worlds have Tuesdays. They have the slow afternoon when nothing happens at the market and the baker's kid is bored and picking at the caulking between the cobblestones. If your world doesn't have boring days, it doesn't have real ones either.

You don't need to know everything about your world. You need to know what your character would notice walking down the street. A soldier scans for sight lines and exits. A chef clocks what's growing in the window boxes, and a child notices the dog. Point of view is a worldbuilding filter that saves you from having to describe everything by forcing you to describe only what matters to the person standing there.

China Miéville's Perdido Street Station has a neighborhood called Spatters where the buildings are made from the calcified ribs of some long-dead giant. He doesn't explain the giant. He doesn't give you three paragraphs of history. The ribs are just there, and they're someone's apartment walls now, and that single detail tells you this is a city that absorbs its own past without sentimentality. One image. An entire philosophy of urban life.

Readers don't notice consistent worldbuilding. They notice inconsistent worldbuilding. This is an underappreciated asymmetry. You'll never get a five-star review that says "the trade routes were plausible." But you will absolutely get a one-star review that says "why would a desert culture waste water on decorative fountains." Consistency is invisible when it's working.

The most common worldbuilding mistake I see is too much of the wrong detail. Three pages on the monarchy's succession laws when the story is about two sisters running a bakery. The daily practice of asking "would my character care about this" would eliminate half the worldbuilding bloat in most fantasy drafts.

Gene Wolfe built the world of The Book of the New Sun almost entirely through vocabulary. He didn't describe the technology of a far-future Earth. He just used words like "fuligin" for a color darker than black and "destrier" for a mount that isn't quite a horse. The reader assembles the world from linguistic texture alone. It's the most economical worldbuilding I've ever encountered and probably the hardest to imitate.

Music, holidays, and children's games. These three things exist in every real culture that has ever existed. If your fictional world doesn't have at least a passing mention of one of them, it'll feel like a set rather than a place. You don't need to describe the whole festival. You need the sound of drums two streets over while your protagonist is trying to concentrate.

A worldbuilding template is only useful until it becomes a trap. I've seen writers fill out forty-page world bibles and never start the novel because the bible kept growing, kept demanding one more section, one more map, one more language chart. The template should be a door you walk through, not a room you move into.

The world reveals itself one writing session at a time. You sit down on a Tuesday morning, and your character turns a corner, and suddenly you know what the lampposts are made of and who lights them and why that job is considered shameful. You couldn't have planned that. The checklist got you to the corner. The writing got you around it. That's how it works, session after session, and the world gets realer each time you show up.
