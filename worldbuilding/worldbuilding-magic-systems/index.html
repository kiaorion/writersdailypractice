How to Create a Magic System (When You Don't Know Where to Start)

Kia Orion | March 6, 2026 | 10 min read

Brandon Sanderson was twenty-three years old and unpublished when he wrote Elantris. The magic system he built for it, called AonDor, worked like this: characters drew luminous symbols in the air, and those symbols channeled power. Elegant. Visually striking. The kind of thing that photographs well on a book cover.

But the thing that made Elantris actually work as a novel had nothing to do with what the magic could do. The entire premise hinged on the fact that AonDor had stopped working. The city of Elantris, once glorious and full of godlike beings, had gone dark. Nobody knew why. The plot, the politics, the character arcs, all of it grew from that single crack in the system.

Sanderson went on to formalize his instincts about magic system design into what he calls his Three Laws of Magic. They've become probably the most referenced framework in modern fantasy writing. Writers quote them in workshops. Reddit threads dissect them endlessly. But what I find more interesting than the laws themselves is the origin. He didn't sit down and theorize about magic design first, then write a novel to prove the theory. He wrote a story about a broken system, felt the story working, and figured out why afterward.

That sequence matters more than people give it credit for.

The best magic systems don't start with the question "what cool things can my characters do." They start from the other direction. What can't they do. What does the magic take from them. Where does it fail.

If you're sitting at your desk trying to design a magic system from scratch and feeling stuck, it's probably because you're starting from the wrong end of the problem. You're building capabilities when you should be building costs.

Hard magic, soft magic, and the spectrum between

Sanderson coined the terms "hard magic" and "soft magic," though the underlying idea is older than his taxonomy. Hard magic means the reader understands the rules clearly. When a character uses magic to solve a problem, the reader can follow the logic, verify it, even predict it. Soft magic means the system stays mysterious. The reader feels its presence without fully understanding its boundaries.

The cleanest example of hard magic I know of is Patrick Rothfuss's sympathy system in The Name of the Wind. Sympathy works on a principle borrowed straight from physics: conservation of energy. If you want to make something move, you need a source of heat or kinetic energy to draw from. You create a sympathetic link between two objects, and energy transfers from one to the other, with some lost to entropy along the way. Kvothe, sitting in his university classes, learns that moving a coin across a table by sympathy requires more energy than just pushing it with your finger. The magic is, thermodynamically speaking, worse than doing things the normal way.

I'm not sure why that detail works so well, but it does. Maybe because it makes the magic feel honest. There's something almost deflating about a system where magic is less efficient than your own hands, and that deflation makes the moments when sympathy does something extraordinary feel genuinely earned. Rothfuss spends pages on the mechanics. The binding types. The Alar, which is basically the strength of your mental conviction that two things are linked. When Kvothe does something remarkable with sympathy later, you understand exactly what it costs him, because you sat through the same lectures he did.

On the other end, you have Tolkien. What can Gandalf actually do? He fights the Balrog. He breaks a bridge with his staff. He arrives at dawn on the fifth day. He seems to mostly just give speeches and smoke a pipe and know things. The system, if there is one, stays behind a curtain. And that's fine, because Gandalf isn't solving plot problems with his magic. He's a guide. The hobbits do the real work.

Ursula K. Le Guin's Earthsea sits in a fascinating middle zone. Naming has power. If you know the true name of the wind, you can call it. If you know the true name of a person, you hold something over them. The rules are clear in the abstract but blurry at the edges, where the system dissolves into philosophy about the nature of language and identity. That blurriness is the point. Le Guin wasn't building a game mechanic. She was writing about what it means to truly know something.

Most working magic systems live somewhere between Rothfuss and Tolkien, and that's fine. The question isn't where yours falls on the spectrum. The question is whether the level of detail you've chosen serves the story you're telling. If you're writing something where characters need to solve problems with magic, build harder. If the magic is atmosphere and wonder, build softer. If you're designing game-mechanic magic for a LitRPG, you'll obviously lean hard. There's no wrong answer except the one that doesn't match.

Every magic system is a cost structure

Here's a question that will clarify your magic system faster than any worldbuilding worksheet: what does the magic take?

Physical toll. Fatigue, pain, nosebleeds, shortened lifespan, accelerated aging. Emotional toll. Lost memories, fractured relationships, creeping madness. Material cost. Rare ingredients consumed in the casting, ancient artifacts that degrade with use, blood sacrifice. The categories matter less than the principle underneath them: magic that costs nothing produces stories about nothing.

When everything is free, when characters can wave their hands and fix problems without consequence, conflict doesn't just weaken. It evaporates. You end up writing longer and longer action sequences to generate tension that the magic system itself keeps dissolving.

Lev Grossman understood this as well as anyone. In The Magicians, becoming a magician requires a specific kind of intelligence, the hyper-analytical, obsessive kind that notices tiny details and holds vast amounts of information simultaneously. The students at Brakebills aren't special because they're brave or good or chosen. They're special because they're smart in a very particular, very narrow way. And that narrow intelligence comes packaged with something Grossman treats as inseparable from it: emotional numbness. Quentin Coldwater can do extraordinary magic precisely because he processes the world through abstraction rather than feeling. He holds the complexity because he's dissociated from the warmth. And that emotional numbness, that distance from his own life and the people in it, is what makes him both powerful and broken, both capable of astonishing feats and incapable of being happy in the room he's standing in. The cost isn't a nosebleed. The cost is who he is as a person.

Robin Hobb did something similar across the entire Realm of the Elderlings series, though she split the cost into two currencies. The Skill, the royal magic, lets users reach into other minds, communicate across distances, shape reality itself at its highest levels. But it's addictive. Users get pulled toward a blissful merging with others, a dissolution of the self that feels like euphoria and looks, from the outside, like a slow vanishing. Fitz spends books fighting the pull. The Wit, the other magic, connects users to animals on a soul-deep level. The cost there is social: Witted folk are despised, hunted, killed. The magic itself is natural and healthy, but the world punishes you for having it. Two systems, two completely different cost structures, both generating decades of story.

If you're writing urban fantasy specifically, the cost question gets even sharper, because modern settings make the absence of consequence more obvious. But the principle holds across every genre. Figure out what your magic takes, and you'll know what your story is about.

Design the edges, not the center

New writers tend to start magic system design by listing what magic can do. Fire spells. Healing spells. Teleportation. A catalog of abilities. It feels productive because you're generating material, filling pages in a notebook or a wiki doc.

But the abilities aren't where stories live. Stories live in the gaps. In the things a character can almost do but not quite, the boundaries where the system starts to buckle.

Go back to Rothfuss. Kvothe can do sympathy. He's good at it, genuinely talented. But sympathy is small-scale, mechanical, rooted in physics. Naming is where the real power sits. And Kvothe can't name. Or rather, he can, but only in flashes, only when something in him cracks open involuntarily and the name of the wind comes rushing through before he can think about it. He can't summon it at will. He can't study his way to it, even though studying is the thing he's best at. His teacher Elodin tells him to stop trying so hard, which is essentially useless advice for someone whose entire identity is built on trying hard and being clever.

That gap, between the magic Kvothe can control and the magic he can't, is what drives two entire books and counting. Every important scene in The Name of the Wind and The Wise Man's Fear orbits that gap. When he calls the wind against Ambrose, it's thrilling because we know he doesn't fully understand how he did it. When he fails to call it during moments of real danger, it's devastating because we know the capability is in there. The limitation creates both the tension and the longing.

Compare that to magic systems with no ceiling. Comics have wrestled with this for decades. Superman can do anything, survive anything, lift anything, and the writers have spent seventy years inventing workarounds, kryptonite and magic vulnerability and red sun radiation, to manufacture the limitations that should have been built in from the start. The edges of the system are where the drama is. If you design a system with no edges, you'll spend your whole career trying to retroactively install them.

When you sit down to work on your magic system, start with the "can't." What does the system refuse to do? Where does it break down? What does your character reach for and miss? The center of the system, the cool stuff, will fill in on its own. The edges are what need your attention.

Let the system grow with the story

There's a particular kind of anxiety that hits writers who are building secondary worlds. The feeling that you need to have the entire magic system mapped before you write the first chapter. Every rule codified. Every edge case accounted for. A full taxonomy of abilities and costs and exceptions, organized in a spreadsheet, before you're allowed to open the draft.

J.K. Rowling didn't do this.

In Philosopher's Stone, wand mechanics are simple. You wave the wand, you say the words, the spell happens. There's some stuff about wand woods and cores, but it's flavor. By Deathly Hallows, wand loyalty has become one of the most important plot mechanics in the entire series. The Elder Wand follows a complex chain of allegiance that runs through duels, disarmings, and a murder that technically didn't count as a defeat. The resolution of the final confrontation with Voldemort hinges on a technicality of wand ownership that Rowling couldn't possibly have planned in 1995.

The Horcrux rules emerged the same way. In Chamber of Secrets, Tom Riddle's diary is just a sinister object. By Half-Blood Prince, it's retroactively become the first Horcrux Harry ever encountered, and the rules around Horcrux creation, the requirement of murder, the splitting of the soul, the question of how many pieces a soul can survive being cut into, have become the spine of the entire endgame. Rowling built backward from where the story needed to go.

This should be a relief. You don't need the whole system before you start. You need enough to write the next scene. Maybe you know that magic has a physical cost but you haven't decided exactly what that cost looks like yet. Fine. Write the scene where your character uses magic for the first time and see what feels right. Does she stagger? Does she forget something? Does she lose an hour of time? The scene will tell you things that the spreadsheet won't.

If you're interested in how magic systems interact with pacing and romance, the same principle applies there too. Let the system reveal itself as the relationship reveals itself. The reader doesn't need the full architecture up front. They need to trust that the architecture exists, and you build that trust one consistent scene at a time.

The magic system reveals itself the way any piece of writing reveals itself: one session at a time, one scene at a time, in the accumulation of small decisions that eventually become a structure you can see. You don't need the whole thing mapped before tomorrow morning. You need to write the scene where the magic works. Or the scene where it doesn't. Or the scene where the cost comes due and your character has to decide whether it's worth paying.
