What changes how your writing reads

Five craft skills worth practicing

Point of view determines everything the reader can see.

Deep POV puts readers inside a character's skull. The narrator disappears. The prose thinks the way the character thinks. Hilary Mantel used it for Wolf Hall and won two Booker Prizes. The technique is learnable, and it starts with cutting two words: "she noticed."

Showing makes the reader feel. Telling makes them read.

The difference is between naming an emotion and building it through body language, objects, and dialogue subtext. In Jesus' Son, Denis Johnson never names what the narrator feels. The body tells the story anyway. That single move, deleting the emotion word and writing the body instead, changes everything.

Tension lives in what you withhold.

Hitchcock's bomb under the table: two people talking about baseball while the audience knows there's a bomb they can't see. Fifteen seconds of surprise versus fifteen minutes of suspense. Tension lives in the gap between what the reader knows and what the character knows.

Dialogue reveals what characters won't say.

The conversation underneath the conversation. In The Remains of the Day, Ishiguro writes two people who are clearly in love and never once say so. They discuss staff management and flower arrangements. The reader fills the silence, and that silence is what makes the novel devastating.

Revision is where craft actually lives.

The first draft is for figuring out what you're trying to say. Everything after that is craft. Cutting the telling word. Placing the object that carries the emotion. Slowing down the scene that needs more silence. These are sentence-level decisions, and they're the difference between prose that works and prose that doesn't.

These skills develop through daily practice, one sentence at a time.

A sample from your daily email

February 3rd

STAY TRUE

"In writing, habit seems to be a much stronger force than either willpower or inspiration."

- John Steinbeck

Steinbeck's point is simple: consistency does more for a writer than inspiration ever will. Those who rely solely on inspiration find themselves at the mercy of its unpredictable nature.

Habit is a form of discipline that transcends the whims of mood and motivation. By establishing a routine, you create a framework for the work. You train your mind to focus, to settle, to go deeper than you'd go on a good day. The more consistent you are, the more willing you become to take risks on the page.

Design your environment to minimize distractions and support your focus. Set a schedule that aligns with your natural rhythms and stick to it. Remember that each session, whether inspired or mundane, contributes to your growth as a writer. Inspiration may spark the flame, but habits keep the fire burning.

Frequently asked questions

Writing craft is the set of techniques that control how prose lands on the reader. Point of view, showing versus telling, tension, dialogue, pacing, revision. These aren't rules you memorize. They're skills you develop through practice, the way a musician develops an ear. Hilary Mantel's deep POV in Wolf Hall, Toni Morrison's body-as-text in Beloved, and Kazuo Ishiguro's subtext-driven dialogue in The Remains of the Day are all examples of craft operating at the highest level.

Read with attention to how sentences work, not just what they say. Pick one technique and practice it in isolation. If you want to improve at showing instead of telling, search your draft for emotion words like "angry" or "sad" and replace each one with a physical action the character's body would perform. Denis Johnson and Toni Morrison both built careers on this single move. Craft improves through repetition, not inspiration.

It means building an emotion or a character trait through concrete detail instead of naming it. "She was sad" is telling. Writing the way she moves through the house, touches an object that belonged to someone she lost, or avoids looking at a photograph is showing. The reader assembles the emotion from evidence rather than being handed a label. Tim O'Brien's The Things They Carried shows Lieutenant Cross's obsessive love through an inventory of objects he carries. The word "love" barely appears.

Tension grows from what you withhold. Alfred Hitchcock explained it best: two people talking with a bomb under the table the audience can see but the characters can't. That gap between what the reader knows and what the character knows creates unbearable suspense from an ordinary conversation. Shirley Jackson's The Lottery, Donna Tartt's The Secret History, and Kazuo Ishiguro's Never Let Me Go all generate tension through restraint and uneven information rather than action.
