Show Don't Tell: What It Actually Looks Like on the Page

Kia Orion | March 6, 2026 | 10 min read

Every writing teacher on earth has said it. Show, don't tell. They say it like it's a light switch you flip. Like you just decide to show and then suddenly you're doing it.

The problem isn't that writers don't understand the concept. The problem is that nobody shows you what showing actually looks like, mechanically, on the page. What changes between the sentence that tells and the sentence that shows. Where the verbs go. What disappears.

There are a few techniques worth knowing. The writers who use them well rarely talk about them in interviews. They just do the thing, and you feel it without knowing why.

1. Let the body do the talking

Words like "angry," "sad," and "afraid" label an emotion the way a museum placard labels a painting. You get the category, but you miss the thing itself.

The fix is absurdly simple: delete the emotion word and write what a camera would record. What the body is doing. The hands, the jaw, the way weight shifts from one foot to the other.

In Denis Johnson's Jesus' Son, the narrator almost never names what he feels. He can't. He's too high, too confused, too deep inside his own wreckage. But his body keeps telling the story anyway. He touches things he shouldn't touch. He stands in places no one should stand. He describes physical sensations with the precision of someone who only has his nerve endings left. You know exactly what he's feeling because he never uses the word for it.

Toni Morrison does something different but related in Beloved. Sethe's body carries what her mind refuses to hold. The scars on her back. The way she moves through the house. Morrison doesn't write "Sethe was traumatized." She writes Sethe's body into every scene like a second text running underneath the dialogue, and you read both at once.

Here's a test you can run on any draft: search for emotion words. Angry, sad, happy, afraid, nervous, excited. Every one you find is a place where you told instead of showed. Delete the word. Write the body instead. Sometimes that's all it takes.

2. The object that carries the weight

Tim O'Brien's The Things They Carried is the clearest example of this technique I've ever read, and I'm not sure why more people don't steal from it directly.

Lieutenant Jimmy Cross carries two photographs of Martha and a pebble she sent him from a beach. He carries them in his rucksack through Vietnam. O'Brien doesn't write "Cross was in love and it distracted him from the war." He inventories what the man carries. Letters. A pebble. The weight in ounces. And through the accumulation of those objects, you understand everything about Cross's interior life without O'Brien ever opening the door and walking you through it.

The technique is: give the emotion to an object. Let the object sit in the scene. Let the character interact with it. The reader does the rest.

Marilynne Robinson does this in Gilead with light itself. Reverend Ames is dying, and he knows it, and the way he describes sunlight falling on a table or catching the edge of his son's hair tells you everything about what it means to be looking at the world for what might be the last time. He never says "I'm dying and the world has become beautiful because of it." He just keeps describing light on surfaces with an attention that only a person running out of time would have.

3. What the character notices reveals who they are

Put two people in the same room. One notices the crack in the ceiling. The other notices the host hasn't made eye contact since they arrived. Same room, completely different stories, and you've shown who each person is without a single line of exposition.

George Orwell's "Shooting an Elephant" is built on this. The narrator is a British police officer in Burma, and the thing that makes the essay devastating is what he pays attention to. He fixates on the crowd behind him. On the size of the elephant. On the mechanical details of the rifle. He notices everything except what he's actually feeling, which is moral horror at what he's about to do. The gap between what he reports and what he avoids tells the whole story. You understand his crisis because of where his eyes go.

Ian McEwan's Atonement takes this even further. Briony is thirteen, and everything she sees gets filtered through her need to make things into stories. She watches two people at a fountain and constructs a narrative that's entirely wrong, and McEwan shows you both what happened and what Briony made of it by controlling what she notices and what she ignores. Her selective attention is the engine of the entire novel's tragedy.

I'm not sure why this works as well as it does. Maybe because in real life we already read people this way. We watch where someone's eyes go at a dinner party and we know something about them they didn't intend to share.

4. Dialogue that shows what the conversation is actually about

Most real conversations are about something other than what's being said. You know this. You've sat through a dinner where two people discussed the parking situation and everyone at the table understood they were actually discussing the affair.

Kazuo Ishiguro builds an entire novel on this in The Remains of the Day. Stevens the butler and Miss Kenton work together for years, and they are clearly, obviously, painfully in love with each other, and they never once say so. They talk about staff management. About the arrangement of flowers. About the proper way to serve at table. And every conversation vibrates with what neither of them will say, and it's one of the most romantic and heartbreaking things I've read because Ishiguro trusts the gap between the words and the meaning, trusts the reader to stand in that gap and feel what the characters won't articulate.

Harold Pinter built an entire career on this. He'd write "pause" into his stage directions the way other playwrights wrote dialogue. The pause was the dialogue. What isn't said, and the silence where it should have been.

When you're writing dialogue, ask what the character wants to avoid saying, and what they talk about instead.

Show don't tell gets taught as a rule, but it's really a collection of small, learnable moves. Put the emotion in the body. Put the feeling in an object. Filter the scene through what the character notices, and let the dialogue circle around the thing nobody will name.

None of this requires talent. It requires practice. It requires sitting with a draft and asking, sentence by sentence, "did I name the thing, or did I build it so the reader could find it themselves?"
